The city and its occupants are improving the efficiency of lighting. Compact fluorescents have replaced many incandescent bulbs. LEDs have replaced old traffic lights, and as they improve and become less expensive, high-efficiency LEDs are replacing halogens and fluorescents too.

For decades utilities have motivated commercial and industrial enterprises to level power consumption by offering lower bulk energy rates with an extra demand charge and time-of-day metering. In response, customers install controls that shut off selected equipment when consumption approaches a demand threshold or that schedule operations for favorable times of day.

In 2010, Lawrence's utility began installing residential "smart meters." Instead of measuring just total energy consumed per month, smart meters transmit frequent samples of instantaneous power back to the utility, enabling the utility to apply demand charges or time-of-day metering to residential customers. The local utility promotes this practice by allowing customers to use the internet to review their consumption histories and alter their practices to minimize their expenses.

Also available are energy controls that operate entirely inside a residence. These devices require more attention, but they provide more privacy and enable residents to specify scheduling and/or temperature control of individual loads like HVAC, lighting, and appliances to reduce consumption and minimize demand or high time-of-day charges.

Also available are "power conditioners," which protect sensitive electronic equipment from voltage spikes, frequency variation, and other pathological phenomena appearing on the power grid. This protects the local facility and improves the value of locally generated electric power.
